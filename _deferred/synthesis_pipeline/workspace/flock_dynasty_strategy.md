## 1. Draft Capital & Landing Spot

### Core Principles
- **Treat NFL draft capital as the single most important variable** for all positions. Round and pick number signal job security, opportunity volume, and organizational investment. [DYNASTY ONLY]
- Use draft capital as **confirmation**, not override — it does not erase medical red flags or poor athletic profiles.
- **Update dynasty rankings immediately** after each day of the NFL Draft. Landing spot impacts are most exploitable before leaguemates identify them.
- Weight NFL draft capital changes **more heavily than pre-draft expectations** when adjusting post-draft rankings.

### By Position
- **Running backs** selected in the top 10 of the NFL Draft historically become first-round dynasty starters — treat them as tier-one assets. [DYNASTY ONLY]
- **Wide receivers** require both draft capital AND situation fit. Top draft capital alone does not guarantee WR success — confirm with QB stability and target opportunity.
- **Tight ends** with top draft capital plus elite athleticism deserve investment despite limited college production, due to positional scarcity.
- **Quarterbacks** taken with top-10 NFL draft capital are speculative dynasty assets worth late-round selections, as they typically earn starting opportunities by year two. [DYNASTY ONLY]
- Wait on quarterbacks taken in round three or later — historical hit rates are extremely low.

## 2. Age & Dynasty Timeline

### Running Backs [DYNASTY ONLY]
- **Core window: Ages 22–26.** Prioritize youth with extended productive runway over aging veterans at the same tier.
- Sell aging RBs (27+) on rebuilding teams even with elite recent production — younger alternatives will outlast them.
- Only roster RBs age 27+ on **contending teams**; avoid them entirely during rebuilds.
- Sell RBs entering age 28+ in **February**, before the NFL Draft introduces competition.
- Be cautious holding RBs entering their age 30+ season — historical data shows sharp production decline and increased injury rates.
- Treat 16+ PPG performances by age-30 RBs as **sell-high opportunities**, not indicators of sustained excellence.

### Wide Receivers [DYNASTY ONLY]
- Target young WRs (ages 22–25) with established target share over 25% as foundational assets.
- Trade away or fade WRs aged 30+ unless you are an **immediate contender**.
- WRs entering ages 27–29 often see production increases — don't sell this window too early.
- Buy low on young WRs (ages 22–23) with disappointing rookie seasons if they had strong prospect profiles.

### Tight Ends [DYNASTY ONLY]
- Avoid aging TEs (30+) in rebuilding dynasties unless Hall of Fame caliber (e.g., Travis Kelce).
- Target rookie TEs on rebuilding rosters for long-term value (e.g., Loveland, Warren-tier prospects).
- Trade declining veteran TEs aggressively once historical comps suggest imminent age-related drops.

### Quarterbacks [DYNASTY ONLY]
- Target young QBs (ages 22–26) who have already demonstrated top-12 fantasy production.
- Trade away aging QBs (33+) if rebuilding, regardless of current production.
- Avoid investing in aging veteran QBs (35+) unless filling a short-term contention window.

## 3. Roster Construction

### General [DYNASTY ONLY]
- Align your roster strategy to your **competitive window**: contenders hold proven performers; rebuilders accumulate youth and picks.
- Prioritize **two top-tier QBs** early in Superflex startup drafts — it creates a lasting competitive advantage.
- Avoid rostering multiple TEs from the same team unless one is a clear starter.
- Do not acquire backup/unproven QBs at the cost of premium rookie picks — add QB depth during the regular season instead.

### Running Backs
- Value receiving upside in RBs — modern NFL offenses increasingly reward pass-catching ability. [REDRAFT APPLICABLE]
- Prioritize red-zone usage over volume when comparing RBs in the same backfield. [REDRAFT APPLICABLE]
- Monitor team RB draft capital — multiple RB selections in consecutive drafts signal reduced workload for your starter. [DYNASTY ONLY]

### Wide Receivers
- Prioritize WRs attached to reliable, long-term starting QBs over those with QB uncertainty.
- Avoid WRs in run-first offenses or with uncertain QB situations regardless of individual talent.
- Handcuff WRs to high-volume QB situations; avoid those in crowded target trees.

### Tight Ends
- Chase **volume** in PPR leagues as the primary indicator of TE success.
- Favor TEs with stable, elite QB play on their roster.
- Avoid TEs in run-first offenses or poor passing volume situations regardless of pedigree.
- Stash unproven TEs with draft capital on bad offenses via taxi squads — acquire cheaply, develop long-term. [DYNASTY ONLY]

## 4. Trade Strategy

### Timing [DYNASTY ONLY]
- Execute rookie pick trades **before, during, or immediately after your league's rookie draft** when market activity is highest.
- Sell aging elite players for future first-round picks **early** — don't wait for mid-season performance spikes.
- Trade aging RBs immediately after **career-best seasons** before age-related decline manifests.

### Selling
- Sell veteran RBs (28+) in **February** before the NFL Draft creates competition.
- Sell WRs aged 27+ on rebuilding teams to acquire younger assets and draft capital, even if production hasn't declined.
- Trade contending-window backs (ages 27–29) if you're rebuilding, even after top-12 finishes.
- Aggressively trade declining veteran TEs once historical comps confirm the age-based drop is coming.
- Trade veteran WRs for mid-to-late rookie picks as a rebuilder when their dynasty value has minimal room to grow.

### Buying
- Buy low on young WRs (ages 22–23) with disappointing rookie seasons if prospect profiles were strong.
- Exploit market inefficiency by trading for established RBs whose value dropped from draft uncertainty **before teams reveal true backfield investments**.
- Target veteran RBs whose teams **did not add competition** in the draft — their value and workload will likely increase.
- Buy low on QBs with poor efficiency metrics if they have significantly improved coaching and supporting casts.

### Rookie Picks [DYNASTY ONLY]
- Only trade away future first-round picks if your roster is a **confirmed top-1, top-2, or top-3 contender** by power rankings.
- Do not trade premium early rookie picks (1.05–1.10) for current-season underperforming veterans.
- Third and fourth-round rookie picks have historically low hit rates — do not value them equally to first-rounders.
- Avoid trading up more than a few spots unless the prospect difference represents a **clear tier break**.
- Use a dynasty trade calculator before accepting any offer — do not undervalue assets blindly.
- Hold picks 106–107 until after the NFL Draft to avoid trading for RBs in uncertain landing situations.
- Trade picks 108–109 safely for recently-signed established RBs before the draft confirms depth charts.

## 5. Rookie Draft Strategy

### Running Backs
- First-round RBs in rookie drafts historically hit at an **86% clip** — they are the best bet available. [DYNASTY ONLY]
- Second-round RBs are the second-best historical investment in rookie drafts. [DYNASTY ONLY]
- Generational RB prospects hit RB1 seasons **100% of the time** — trade up for them rather than waiting.
- **Avoid drafting RBs who are both slow (sub-4.63 forty) AND small (under 214 lbs)** — historical data shows virtually zero fantasy relevance.
- Do not reach on late-round/day-three RBs based on year-one performance in mediocre offenses. [DYNASTY ONLY]

### Wide Receivers
- **Avoid third-round WRs** due to historically poor hit rates — pivot to other positions. [DYNASTY ONLY]
- Do not draft WRs with fewer than 25% target share and fewer than 2.5 yards per route run unless they played alongside multiple NFL-caliber receivers.
- Avoid WRs under 185 lbs unless they have elite production profiles or exceptional testing in compensating metrics.
- Discount production metrics for WRs who competed alongside multiple NFL-caliber receivers in college.

### Tight Ends
- Target TEs drafted in round three or later who **grade well in prospect models** — they hit at decent rates. [DYNASTY ONLY]
- Evaluate TEs on athleticism and movement skills heavily, as college production translates inconsistently.
- Require TEs to test above **6.5 RAS score** — elite athleticism is essential to the position's fantasy relevance.

### Quarterbacks
- Prioritize QBs in Superflex formats with **clear paths to starting**, even in weak draft classes. [DYNASTY ONLY]
- Use top-10 NFL draft capital for QBs as a strong speculative signal for year-two starting opportunity.
- Accumulate backup QB prospects in Superflex leagues — starting opportunities are valuable and unpredictable.

### General Rookie Draft Rules
- Target players where **veteran starters are on expiring contracts** or aging out.
- In weak draft classes, select **high-conviction evaluations** over chasing hit-rate statistics that become unreliable.
- Exploit positional converts (especially WR-to-RB) who may earn early playing time.
- Avoid high-profile prospects who showed **significant statistical decline** in their final college season.
- Do not overweight a single impressive game — review full season performance.
- Recognize that strong **playoff performances can artificially inflate** prospect value — prioritize sustained production.

## 6. College & Prospect Evaluation

### Running Backs
- Prioritize RBs with **7+ yards per carry** efficiency in college. [DYNASTY ONLY]
- Value demonstrated size (6'0"+, 220+ lbs) as an indicator of NFL team interest and workload potential.
- Do not discount RBs for lacking receiving usage **if generating elite rushing efficiency**.
- Prefer RBs showing **improved efficiency year-over-year** over those declining despite similar opportunity.
- Prioritize high missed tackle force rates over draft pedigree or athletic testing.
- Favor RBs who maximize efficiency on every touch over those dependent on volume or O-line performance.
- Target RBs with exceptional lateral agility and vision even without elite straight-line speed.

### Wide Receivers
- Prioritize college production metrics (yards per route run, target share, dominator rating) over landing spot in initial evaluations.
- **Hard floor:** Sub-2.3 career yards per route run + sub-0.1 first downs per route run = discount regardless of draft capital or testing.
- Discount prospects who cannot generate separation and rely heavily on contested catches.
- Downgrade WRs entering the NFL at **23+ years old** compared to younger prospects with similar production.
- Avoid overweighting late-season breakout performances — assess whether production was recurring or circumstantial.
- Be skeptical of receivers whose production came primarily against man coverage in their final college season after limited early-career volume.

### Tight Ends
- Evaluate TEs primarily on **film traits and movement skills** rather than college production alone.
- Target TEs with smooth route-breaking ability and exceptional yards after catch rather than blocking prowess.
- Immediately deploy TEs with receiving skill sets in pass-heavy roles regardless of blocking limitations.

### Quarterbacks
- Use **adjusted yards per attempt** as a more reliable measure than total fantasy points or raw efficiency.
- Separate real-world NFL QB ability from fantasy production — efficient passers may not generate top fantasy points.

## 7. Combine & Athletic Testing

- Use **RAS (Relative Athletic Score) minimum thresholds as red flags** to eliminate prospects, not as predictors of stardom.
- Do not use combine outliers (Antonio Brown, Tom Brady) to justify drafting similarly profiled prospects — they are statistical impossibilities.
- Combine data confirms or eliminates; it does not **upgrade** players who already qualified on film and production.
- Prospects whose film evaluation **matches** their combine results offer the highest investment confidence.
- Monitor how combine performances shift draft capital — poor testing can dramatically lower draft position and opportunity.
- Move up rankings for prospects who **confirm anticipated athleticism** at the combine, particularly vertical threats.
- Account for the overall athletic depth of an incoming class before adjusting dynasty valuations up or down.
- Evaluate receiving upside (7.5+ yards per reception in college) as a predictive indicator of NFL receiving role potential.

## 8. In-Season Management

### Waiver Wire / Depth Charts [REDRAFT APPLICABLE]
- Use **snap count percentage (60%+)** as a primary threshold for projecting WR production.
- Monitor team-wide receiver room changes and adjust remaining receivers' valuations upward when competing receivers depart.
- Monitor rookie RB draft capital added by your team in-season — new day-two picks create committee risk for current starters.

### Injury Management [DYNASTY ONLY]
- **Avoid RBs returning from Achilles injuries** — limited historical precedent exists for full recovery at the position.
- Sell or downgrade players suffering major injuries (ACL, knee) until they demonstrate on-field production upon return.
- Hold injured lottery-ticket receivers unless offered a first-round pick — future draft capital is unreliable.
- Avoid WRs recovering from ACL injuries in rookie drafts due to training camp/early-season availability uncertainty.
- Discount injury-prone QBs by applying a ranking penalty even when per-game production is elite.

### Competitive Intelligence
- Use **real ADP data from completed community drafts** (e.g., Sleeper) rather than platform rankings, which are often outdated or inflated.
- Search for and manually target undervalued players listed too low on Sleeper before they reach their real ADP.
- Pass on players ranked highly on platform rankings but with much later real ADP in actual drafts.
- Downgrade off-field red flag players below similar-tier players with clean histories, even if on-field production is comparable.

## 9. Situational & Scheme Factors

- **Offensive coordinator and QB changes** are primary drivers of WR valuation shifts — weight them heavily. [REDRAFT APPLICABLE]
- Prioritize RBs in **pass-heavy offenses** — but note they may face goal-line vulturing and lower checkdown rates.
- Consider offensive line and coaching stability when evaluating RBs — unproven QBs and poor OL reduce touchdown and efficiency upside.
- In situations where a **star WR may walk in free agency**, the remaining WR in that offense gains significant upside.
- Account for QB mobility when evaluating RB efficiency — dual-threat QBs boost O-line reads but reduce checkdown volume.
- Favor RBs from high-powered offenses — scheme and supporting cast drive consistent scoring.
- Discount WRs in dysfunctional franchise situations (historically: Jets, Browns) regardless of individual talent.

## 10. Conference & Competition Context

- Do **not disqualify non-Power Five performers** with elite efficiency metrics — account for conference strength but don't penalize it categorically.
- Contextualize collegiate production by identifying elite receiving competition in the same offense before dismissing low analytics.
- Fade prospects from **low-volume passing offenses** whose statistics may be artificially depressed by scheme rather than talent.
- Be skeptical of air-raid offense production — do not overweight stats manufactured by system volume.
