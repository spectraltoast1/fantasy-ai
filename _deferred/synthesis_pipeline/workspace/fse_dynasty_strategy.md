## Startup Draft Strategy
*Dynasty-specific unless noted*

### Direction & Commitment
1. **Identify your direction early** — evaluate your league's tendencies, scoring settings, and roster construction before draft day. Run mock drafts to understand positional value in your specific context.
2. **Commit fully to one direction** — be aggressively competitive or aggressively rebuilding. Avoid the middle ground. Teams stuck between both approaches underperform both.
3. **Stay flexible in rounds 1–4** — avoid locking into purely competitive or purely rebuilding assets before you've established your team's natural direction.
4. **Use future picks as startup currency** — rostering rookie picks instead of committing to players delays your directional decision and preserves optionality.

### Pick Strategy
5. **Take the best available player** at every selection rather than drafting for positional need — you can address needs through immediate post-draft trades.
6. **Understand opportunity bundles** — how different position combinations in early rounds affect overall team construction, not just individual pick value.
7. **Use draft capital flexibility both ways** — trade up for premium prospects *and* trade down to accumulate additional year assets rather than rigidly drafting by rank.
8. **In Superflex startups, secure two anchor quarterbacks** in early-to-middle rounds before the QB2 tier collapses. The drop-off after QB10–12 is severe.

### Positional Prioritization
9. **Prioritize "anchor players"** — foundational assets likely to remain elite at their position for multiple years — over upside lottery tickets in early rounds.
10. **Target wide receivers and running backs under 24** who have already demonstrated 20+ PPG seasons as dynasty cornerstones.
11. **Build quarterback flexibility** by not going heavy on QB early in standard formats — the QB10–24 tier has flattened enough to find superior value at skill positions.

> ⚠️ **Conflict Note:** Rule 8 (secure two QBs early in SF) vs. Rule 11 (don't go heavy QB early) — these are format-specific: Rule 8 applies to Superflex, Rule 11 applies to 1QB or shallow SF leagues where the QB tier is less steep.

## Rookie Draft Strategy
*Dynasty-specific*

### Pick Acquisition
1. **Trade up into the top six prospects whenever possible** — tier-one talent is worth the capital cost and the hit rate justifies the premium.
2. **Accumulate volume in the 2.11–3.12 range** — consensus is weakest and value variance is highest here; multiple swings outperform a single early-second pick.
3. **Trade out of weak current classes entirely** for future capital when leaguemates undervalue upcoming classes (e.g., 2027, 2028). Stack where depth is strongest.
4. **In weak draft classes**, accept lower-floor prospects with high-ceiling upside in ambiguous backfields rather than overdrafting safe-floor options.
5. **Evaluate rookie pick trade values contextually** by comparing available talent within each specific class — do not use static year-to-year valuations.

### Draft Execution
6. **Never draft for positional need** — take best available and address gaps through trades with current assets.
7. **Do not outsmart consensus on top-tier players** — trade down only when your rankings align with late-round ADP expectations for your targets.
8. **Make your own mistakes** — maintain conviction in contrarian picks rather than blindly following expert consensus.
9. **Execute multiple incremental trades** rather than swinging for home runs — consistent deal-making compounds advantages over time.
10. **Trade down safely** using ADP landing spot data as confirmation that your targets will still be available at later picks.
11. **Use landing spot as a tiebreaker within tiers**, not as a reason to draft a lesser prospect multiple rounds early.

### Timing
12. **Avoid selling early first-round picks immediately in the offseason** — hold until closer to the draft when free agency reveals landing spot clarity and increases pick value.
13. **Sell end-of-first-round picks (1.10–1.12) proactively** rather than holding, as this range historically underperforms post-NFL draft relative to pre-draft price.
14. **Trade during startup draft preparation** — contact other managers before your picks to facilitate value exchanges rather than making generic post-draft offers.

## Prospect Evaluation
*Dynasty-specific*

### Core Framework
1. **Film evaluation is your primary tool** — do not overweight combine testing results. Rewatch tape when combine performance challenges your film grades.
2. **Prioritize prospect grade over draft capital** — draft capital is a useful tiebreaker and opportunity signal, but not the primary evaluation lens.
3. **Production context matters significantly** — adjust prospect grades based on offensive line quality, surrounding talent, scheme constraints, and opponent quality.
4. **Multi-year college production beats one-year wonders** at every position — consistency and volume better predict sustainable NFL roles.
5. **Competition level matters** — prospects from Power Four schools should be weighted more heavily than Group of Five producers, but elite athleticism from small schools can compensate.

### Draft Capital Usage
6. **Use NFL draft capital as a tiebreaker** between similarly-graded prospects, not as a standalone evaluation driver.
7. **Avoid reaching on prospects solely based on draft capital** — evaluate prospect tier within each round, not just the round itself.
8. **First-round capital deserves a premium at tight end** specifically, as it rarely goes to that position and signals organizational commitment.
9. **Discount prospects with poor landing spots** regardless of draft capital — NFL usage patterns ultimately depend on coaching trust, not draft position.

### Red Flags
10. **Apply significant caution to injury-prone prospects** (especially ACL tears) — recovery timelines often prevent rookie-year skill development.
11. **Avoid prospects with multiple compounding red flags** (off-field concerns + injury history + poor fundamentals) regardless of athletic profile.
12. **Be skeptical of highlight-reel prospects** who look better on cut-up tape than all-22 film — production volume can mask limited skill development.
13. **Discount significant upward revisions in draft capital projections** for small-school prospects following one strong combine — wait for production-based validation.

### Combine & Testing
14. **Use combine performance to validate or challenge film grades** — strong athletic testing can justify higher dynasty investment for trait-dependent prospects.
15. **Monitor pro day results** before finalizing grades on prospects who skipped the combine, especially those with injury concerns.
16. **Evaluate size/weight measurements against listed specs** — discrepancies often signal player priorities that conflict with NFL organizational needs.
17. **Assess medical clearances carefully** — injury recovery status significantly impacts long-term dynasty value regardless of talent grade.

## Position-Specific Evaluation
*Dynasty-specific unless noted*

### Quarterback
1. **Evaluate QBs on a four-year prospect window** (years 1–4), not three — QB development takes longer than other positions.
2. **Assess QB hit rates post-year-two**, not post-year-one, to accurately determine franchise viability.
3. **Prioritize draft capital and landing spot for QBs more heavily than other positions** — it directly determines on-field opportunity.
4. **Target mobile/Konami-archetype QBs over pocket passers** at similar draft costs — rushing yards provide meaningful fantasy floor and dynasty upside.
5. **Prioritize QBs with multi-year starting experience** and larger college samples over one-year starters regardless of physical tools.
6. **Avoid taking one-year starters with under 20 career starts in the first three rounds** unless they are genuinely exceptional prospects.
7. **Weigh quarterback-developer coaching more heavily** than weapons availability when evaluating landing spots.
8. **Target backup QB situations** where the starter has injury risk, age concerns, or contract uncertainty for 1–2 year breakout windows.
9. **Target day-three QB prospects with elite trait grades ("smash" labels)** over day-one QB prospects with limited experience or weak historical comps.
10. **Trade down from early-second-round QB picks** to acquire proven veteran QBs or higher-upside late-round QB prospects.
11. **In 1QB leagues, only draft a QB if your team actually needs one** — avoid taking them based on draft capital or prospect ranking alone.

### Running Back
1. **Prioritize prospect model grades over draft capital alone** when evaluating RB talent.
2. **Second-round NFL draft capital has meaningfully higher hit rates** for immediate starting opportunities than third-round backs.
3. **Avoid third-round or later RBs unless they clearly win the starting job** — hit rates drop significantly below second-round capital.
4. **Target RBs with strong pass-catching ability and pass protection skills** even if straight-line speed tests below ideal.
5. **Deprioritize receiving-only RBs without demonstrated between-the-tackles ability** — coaches won't grant three-down workloads to one-dimensional profiles.
6. **Avoid RBs under 6'0" or under 205 lbs without elite contact balance** — structural obstacles limit full workload regardless of elusiveness.
7. **Weight contact balance, vision, and pass protection** over raw speed and athletic testing when projecting NFL role.
8. **Discount RBs with poor yards-after-contact-per-attempt** even when raw yardage totals appear strong.
9. **Target RBs with multi-year college starting experience** — durability and consistency predict sustainable NFL roles better than single-season explosions.
10. **Examine injury history context carefully** — long-term injuries limiting production volume distort talent evaluation more than they indicate NFL ceiling.
11. **Use weight-adjusted speed scores above 120 as a baseline qualifier** for first-round dynasty rookie draft consideration.
12. **Target younger RBs (age 20–21)** in dynasty — multiple college seasons remaining to develop profile and improve draft stock.

> ⚠️ **Conflict Note:** Multiple sources suggest targeting RBs with clear three-down roles; other sources suggest receiving-back specialists can be targeted in rounds 3–4. These are not mutually exclusive but represent different risk/reward profiles — clarify your league's PPR vs. standard scoring before prioritizing.

### Wide Receiver
1. **Prioritize early-declare true juniors over older prospects** with similar talent — developmental runway is a meaningful dynasty advantage.
2. **Weight film evaluation and on-field production over testing metrics** when significant competition-level gaps exist.
3. **Target receivers with elite contested catch rates (80%+) and strong hands** even when other metrics are concerning.
4. **Value zone coverage ability and route-running consistency over raw athleticism** or YAC potential.
5. **Seek versatility** — receivers who can play multiple alignments reduce landing spot risk substantially.
6. **Discount narrow receivers under 5'10"** in dynasty — the fantasy-relevant track record for sub-5'10" WRs is extremely limited regardless of athleticism.
7. **Discount receivers from scheme-heavy systems** (heavy RPO, tunnel screen-dependent) until they prove skills translate in neutral NFL settings.
8. **Avoid slot-only receivers** unless they land with coaching trees specifically designed to maximize that role.
9. **Fade receivers going to teams with established elite options** unless the current starter is aging out.
10. **Target complementary skill-set receivers** to existing team weapons rather than redundant profiles in crowded corps.
11. **Non-early declares have significantly lower NFL success rates** — apply skepticism to seniors and graduate transfers relative to juniors.
12. **Identify underrated vertical separation ability in taller-frame receivers** as upside leverage often missed in standard evaluations.

### Tight End
1. **Target TEs with elite athleticism (sub-4.45 40-time, top percentile vertical)** even if college production lags — athletic upside correlates with dynasty value.
2. **Prioritize receiving ability over blocking** — fantasy's top TEs operate in the slot regardless of size. Blocking grades are largely irrelevant for dynasty.
3. **Target TEs with top-50 NFL draft capital and clear long-term roles** over lower-capital TEs with better short-term landing spots.
4. **Avoid TEs with average yards-per-reception under 10 and short average depth of target under 6 yards** — this indicates forced production, not real receiving ability.
5. **Value downfield receiving ability and 20+ yard target production** as predictors of ceiling and NFL versatility.
6. **Prioritize hand size, contested catch rate, and drop rate metrics** over blocking grades when evaluating film.
7. **Don't eliminate prospects due to one poor pre-draft process performance** — prioritize film evaluation over combine/pro day results at this position.
8. **In deep leagues, roster early production-based TEs alongside high-ceiling developmental prospects** for roster flexibility.
9. **In TE premium leagues, target athletes with elite size, catch radius, and red zone potential** over production metrics, as these traits translate most predictably.
10. **Don't overdraft TEs in non-TE-premium formats** — adjust valuations down 1–2 rounds compared to Superflex or TEP rankings.

## Trade Strategy
*Dynasty-specific*

### General Principles
1. **Evaluate your leaguemate's team and needs before sending an offer** — not just your own roster construction.
2. **Send fair initial offers (10–20% in your favor)** rather than fleece offers — this encourages counter-offers and builds trade relationships.
3. **Follow every offer with a direct message** explaining your rationale — transparency increases acceptance rates.
4. **Send 25–30 trade offers across your leagues** expecting only 10–15% acceptance — volume and persistence compound into advantages.
5. **Customize offers to match leaguemate situations** — picks and youth for rebuilders, immediate production for contenders.
6. **Send multiple offer variations** showing different paths (rebuild vs. compete) to the same manager.
7. **Follow up rejected offers** with "no worries, let me know if things change" — maintain relationships for future deals.
8. **Never make trades to keep league members happy** — optimize your roster construction and let others manage themselves.
9. **Avoid the sunk cost fallacy** — evaluate and trade players based on current value and future outlook, not draft capital spent.

### Buy Strategy
1. **Target buy-low windows on players with recent negative publicity, injury concerns, or bad situations** — scared managers undersell.
2. **Buy players whose offensive line, quarterback, or coaching staff situation is improving** before the market reflects it.
3. **Buy young players coming off ACL tears in year two of recovery** when market value remains depressed.
4. **Buy talented prospects in suboptimal landing spots when the situation can realistically improve** (QB turnover, depth chart openings in year 2–3).
5. **Buy second and third-round talents with elite college tape** who lacked opportunity — NFL volume in better situations unlocks upside.
6. **Target players whose teams failed to address their position in free agency or the draft** — they gain opportunity value.
7. **Monitor coaching and coordinator changes between leagues** — established players' values shift before the wider market recognizes it.
8. **Patrol free agent signings as buy-low opportunities** — landing spot uncertainty depresses values before confirmation.
9. **Buy receivers entering year two or three in their current offense** before breakout seasons as development curves provide better value.
