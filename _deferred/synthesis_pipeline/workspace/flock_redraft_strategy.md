## 1. Draft Strategy — General Principles

### Positional Priority & Tiering
- Build your roster using a tiered approach: secure the best available player at each tier before moving to the next tier down.
- Use **league-specific draft sheets** that match your exact roster size, scoring format, and starting requirements — never draft from generic ADP alone.
- In **double-flex or multi-flex leagues**, prioritize wide receiver depth in early rounds over traditional positional balance.
- Adjust player rankings based on your league's starting roster requirements — tight ends and quarterbacks gain value in leagues with fewer flex spots.
- Deviate from pre-draft plans when elite value falls outside expected ADP ranges, especially for position-scarce players.

### Quarterback
- **Wait until double-digit rounds** to draft your first QB; grab a second QB at the end of the draft rather than using mid-round picks on tier-four quarterbacks.
- Only target elite QBs (top 4) if they fall to round 3 or later; otherwise wait.
- **[REDRAFT]** In leagues with only two WR spots and one flex, consider an elite QB in round two or three. In multi-flex leagues, push elite QBs to round three or later.
- Target QBs with **rushing upside** in four-point passing TD formats over pure pocket passers.
- Prioritize QBs with young weapons, offensive line improvements, or second-year system familiarity over proven outliers expected to regress.
- Avoid reaching on QBs entering down years due to personnel losses.
- Do not draft tier-four QBs in rounds five or six when tier-two QBs are available two rounds earlier.
- Avoid rostering a second QB until the end of the draft; play matchups weekly instead.

> **⚡ CONFLICT — QB Timing:** One source recommends waiting until double-digit rounds for all QBs regardless of format. Another says elite QBs should be taken in round two or three in two-WR leagues. **Resolution:** The format-specific guidance is more nuanced and likely correct — apply the round two/three rule only in shallow receiver/flex formats; otherwise wait.

### Running Back
- Secure at least **one guaranteed RB1 or high-upside RB1** (top-13 running back) within your first four picks.
- Draft **at least three running backs by round nine** to ensure depth.
- Prioritize RB value in double-digit rounds before wide receiver availability dries up.
- Target backup RBs with injury upside in late rounds as they can become immediate starters.
- **[REDRAFT]** Avoid starting rookie RBs in Week 1 unless they have secured 80%+ snap rate with no meaningful competition.
- Avoid starting running backs in backfields with established committee approaches announced by coaching staff.

### Wide Receiver
- Secure **at least one elite WR by round three**, regardless of other position needs.
- Draft at least **three WRs by round six** before the tier significantly drops.
- Accumulate **at least five WRs by round nine**, as the position becomes unreliable after this point.
- In double-flex leagues, ensure your first four picks include at least two WRs and one RB.
- Avoid drafting WRs from bottom-five NFL offenses — they have only a **26% chance of finishing top-24** compared to 75% for top-five offenses.
- Avoid wide receivers on teams projected for bottom-five scoring regardless of individual talent.

### Tight End
- Only target **elite TEs (top 3)** if they don't force you to skip multiple WRs or RBs in early rounds.
- Avoid drafting tight ends in **middle rounds (5–10)** due to the logarithmic production drop-off; either secure an elite option early or wait until late rounds.
- If you don't secure an elite TE, draft **two mid-tier TEs in separate rounds** rather than one in the middle rounds.
- Do not reach on tight ends in round six; wait for elite options in rounds two through four or accept late-round alternatives.
- Target **rookie TEs with round-one NFL draft capital** in late rounds as high-ceiling lottery tickets.
- Avoid TEs with multiple team changes and limited track record across different offenses.

> **⚡ CONFLICT — TE Draft Timing:** One source says avoid TEs entirely in rounds 5–10. Another says target elite TEs in rounds 2–4. Both agree on avoiding the middle tier. **Resolution:** These are compatible — the rule is: elite TE early (rounds 2–4) OR late-round TE (rounds 11+). The mid-round range is universally discouraged.

## 2. Draft Strategy — Player Evaluation

### Offense & Situation First
- Prioritize **passing volume and offensive strength** over individual receiver talent when evaluating WR value.
- Prioritize players on offenses **projected to score in the top eight** in the NFL.
- Discount all skill position players in **bottom-five projected offenses**, as ceiling constraints are real and persistent.
- Prioritize players with elite QB play and stable offensive environments over similarly talented players in worse situations.

### Age & Trajectory
- **Fade aging players (27+)** at premium positions unless they demonstrated top-tier efficiency and volume in the previous season.
- Apply **age-based regression discounts** to RBs entering their age 30+ seasons, especially those with significant carry history.
- **Sell aging veterans (30+)** before they enter sharp decline phases to maximize trade value.
- Target young, ascending players over aging veterans showing declining athletic metrics, even if veterans had stronger recent seasons.
- Target young WRs (ages 21–23) with rookie seasons comparable to players who improved significantly in year two.
- Target young TEs (ages 22–26) in early rounds, as they typically improve year-over-year.

### Efficiency Metrics
- Prioritize **yards per route run** and **snap percentage** as leading indicators of sustained elite production.
- Prioritize **target share and route participation percentage** over single-week fantasy points.
- Prioritize **air-yard share and target percentage relative to team passing volume** rather than raw target counts.
- Evaluate RBs on **snap percentage and three-down workload potential**, as high-touch players sustain production more reliably.
- Prioritize efficiency metrics (yards per carry, yards per target) over total production when evaluating players in limited roles.
- Use **range-of-outcomes and ceiling/floor analysis** rather than median projections when evaluating young players versus proven veterans.

### Sportsbook & Vegas Data
- Compare sportsbook projections across multiple sources to identify when expert consensus rankings diverge from Vegas consensus — these gaps represent draft value.
- Use **sportsbook implied team totals and Vegas spreads** to evaluate offensive environment, not just season-long projections.
- Target WRs whose sportsbook receiving yardage and TD projections suggest they're undervalued relative to ADP.

## 3. Waiver Wire

### General Philosophy
- **Focus on not ruining your team** through bad waiver decisions rather than chasing lottery ticket pickups.
- Use **league-specific waiver wire analysis tools** rather than general trending lists to identify available players in your specific league.
- Evaluate waiver targets based on your **league's specific roster composition** rather than general consensus rankings.

### Running Backs **[REDRAFT]**
- Target **backup RBs in ambiguous backfield situations** after Week 1 to identify potential league-winning starters.
- **Do not start Week 1 waiver wire pickups** — use the first week to evaluate role and opportunity before deploying them.
- Drop waiver wire RBs immediately if they don't receive the expected role in Week 1.
- **Prioritize rostering RBs over WRs** when choosing between waiver options in early weeks.
- Roster players in **unclear backfield depth charts** to capitalize on potential starting opportunities.
- Target **high-ceiling handcuff RBs** over low-upside depth players to maximize contingency value.
- Target players who **lost competition due to injury** in the previous week, as they typically see immediate role expansion.
- Prioritize high-volume pass-catchers on waivers in weeks following injuries to their competition, even if fantasy output hasn't been elite.
- Avoid picking up WRs returning from injury unless the starter ahead of them is injured or traded away.

### Wide Receivers **[REDRAFT]**
- **Do not overcommit FAB budget** on WRs who had one-week touchdown spikes, as they typically regress to irrelevance.
- Avoid overreacting to WR performances in games with unusual circumstances (four passing TDs, opposing team injuries).
- Prioritize WRs showing **increased snap count and target share trends** over those with single-week TD spikes.
- Avoid rostering WRs in the worst offenses unless they have a clear path to increased role or injured starter replacement.
- Don't waste roster spots on boom-bust WRs with unpredictable TD dependency when streaming options exist.

### Tight Ends **[REDRAFT]**
- **Prioritize TE pickups in Week 1** when all league TEs perform poorly — this is the one position where heavy investment won't be regretted.
- Evaluate TE **snap counts and route participation trends** to identify sustainable role expansion.
- Target TEs with consistent target volume and slot receiver usage over high-draft-capital players in poor matchups.
- Prioritize elite TEs with **90%+ snap participation and 8+ target averages** over streaming options.

### Defense / DST **[REDRAFT]**
- **Stream defenses** by targeting units playing against the lowest-projected scoring offenses with large point spreads.
- Use **sportsbook implied team totals and Vegas spreads** to identify optimal defensive streamers rather than relying on season-long projections.
- Stream defense against the league's lowest-projected scoring offenses rather than relying on drafted defensive players.

### IR Slots
- **Prioritize rostering IR-eligible players in empty IR slots** to gain extra roster flexibility at no cost. **[REDRAFT]**

## 4. Trade Strategy — Buying Low

### General Buy-Low Rules
- **Use trade calculators and trade finders** to validate whether proposed trades represent actual value upgrades before accepting any deal.
- Trade aggressively for players priced below their historical performance range.
- Acquire players whose offensive teammates are being traded away, increasing their target/touch share.
- Monitor **practice reports from team insiders** to confirm recovery timelines beyond official coach statements.

### Wide Receivers
- **Trade high-volume receivers who underperformed in Week 1** due to injury or bad offensive circumstances rather than selling at a loss.
- Identify offenses with historically poor Week 1 performances and buy their players at discounts, as these teams often improve significantly in subsequent weeks.
- Buy receivers from offenses with **elite QBs or playcallers** even after poor Week 1 performances, as volume-based production typically recovers.
- Target WRs with **no target competition** in their offense over those sharing volume.

### Running Backs
- Target **suspended or injured players** whose return timeline is shorter than five weeks, as trade value increases each week closer to eligibility.
- Back RBs whose backfield mate has a **history of fumbles or inconsistency**.
- Acquire RBs gaining **snap share and touch increases week-over-week** as evidence of role expansion.
- Target RBs **returning from injury** who underperformed before getting hurt due to limited snap counts.
- Handcuff premium backup RBs who have demonstrated consistent scoring when the starter misses games.

### Tight Ends
- Upgrade your TE position by **trading depth at WR** to acquire elite, consistently-targeted TEs in PPR leagues.
- Target **injury-plagued TEs in year two** who showed elite efficiency when healthy, as recovery often precedes breakout production. **[DYNASTY leaning]**

## 5. Trade Strategy — Selling High

### General Sell-High Rules
- **Sell players whose production spike is driven by temporary circumstances** (injury to competition, unusual game script) rather than sustainable role changes.
- **Trade away players with unsustainably high TD rates** before regression occurs.
- Reset volume expectations when a player's target/touch share returns to historical norms after a temporary increase.
- Use the **trade calculator before proposing any deal** to ensure fair value exchange.
- **Package underperforming players with recent sell-high candidates** in two-for-one trades to upgrade to more reliable producers.

### Wide Receivers
- Sell WRs who are **30+ years old** coming off significant production declines, as aging receivers rarely bounce back after dramatic falloffs.
- Sell **young receivers whose target share decreased year-over-year**, even if team passing volume remained stable.
- Consider selling second-year receivers who underperformed despite optimal team circumstances.
- Downgrade receivers on teams that **significantly shifted toward run-heavy offensive strategies** in the offseason.
- Trade aging receivers on the decline for **younger receivers with upward trajectory**.

### Running Backs
- Downgrade RBs in offenses that experience **significant offensive line injuries**, as sportsbook projections will reflect the true impact before market prices adjust.
- Sell RBs whose fantasy production was driven primarily by **touchdown variance** rather than consistent volume metrics.
- Trade away starting RBs in **newly created or expanded committees** before the market fully prices in the touch share reduction.
- Reduce valuation for elite prospects when **high-draft-capital backups** gain meaningful practice reps, signaling potential committee usage.
- Sell RBs showing **physical regression indicators** (declining yards per carry, juke rate drops) even if overall fantasy output remains stable.
- Move injured RBs down significantly when team management becomes **"non-committal"** on playing time.
- Trade away **low-volume RBs with limited upside ceilings** as secondary pieces in package deals to upgrade other positions.

## 6. Starting Lineup Decisions **[REDRAFT]**

### Universal Rules
- **Start all tier-one and tier-two players regardless of matchup.** Benching them indicates a flawed league setup.
- Monitor **injury status throughout the week** and adjust rankings daily as new information emerges.
- Update rankings daily based on practice reports and injury developments.
- Do not overweight recent injuries for players **confirmed healthy in Week 1** — start them unless significantly limited in practice.

### Quarterbacks
- Prioritize QBs in **high over-under games**, as sportsbook projections are more accurate than historical performance alone.
- Avoid starting QBs whose teams project as **bottom-five scoring offenses**, even in blowout scenarios.
- Favor QBs with **rushing upside** when comparing similarly-ranked passers.

### Running Backs
- Prioritize RBs with **favorable offensive matchups and high implied team totals** over those in poor game environments, regardless of draft capital.
- Fade RBs in **low-scoring game environments** (implied team totals under 18 points) unless they have elite receiving roles.
- Bench **handcuff RBs** in their first week if the starter is healthy, regardless of handcuff quality.
- Prioritize RBs whose backup/competition missed significant training camp time or is dealing with injury uncertainty.
- **Target RBs in games with massive point spreads** favoring their team, indicating potential second-half rushing volume.
- Avoid starting **third-day draft picks** in backfield committees, especially in poor offensive matchups.
- Start **veterans with established preseason reps** over unproven backfield competitors, even if long-term prospects may be superior.

Another says hold rookie RBs on the bench until they prove efficiency, rather than benching after one poor performance. **Resolution:** Both recommend caution in Week 1. The difference is in weeks 2+: give rookies time to establish a role before abandoning them, but don't force them into your lineup prematurely.

- Start elite WRs (top tier) **regardless of matchup difficulty**.
- Prioritize WRs with **high target share projections** over boom-or-bust big play potential.
